DOMAINLY.AI - STARTUP KNOWLEDGE BASE

=== CORE FRAMEWORKS ===
- MVP (Minimum Viable Product): the smallest version of a product that
  lets you test your core hypothesis with real users.
- Product-Market Fit: the point where demand is strong enough that
  growth becomes primarily a distribution problem, not a demand problem.
- Lean Startup loop: Build -> Measure -> Learn, repeated in short cycles,
  pivoting or persevering based on validated learning.
- Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD): customers "hire" a product to do a job in
  their life; understand the job, not just the demographic.
- Blue Ocean Strategy: compete in uncontested market space instead of
  fighting head-to-head in a saturated one ("red ocean").

=== EARLY-STAGE VALIDATION ===
1. Talk to potential customers before writing code; validate the problem
   exists and is painful enough that people would pay to solve it.
2. Look for "hair on fire" problems - ones people are already trying
   (and paying) to solve, even with bad workarounds.
3. Track a small number of core metrics (activation, retention, referral)
   rather than vanity metrics like total signups or app downloads.
4. A landing page + waitlist or a manual "concierge MVP" (doing the
   service by hand before automating) can validate demand before you
   build anything.
5. Watch what users actually do, not just what they say they'd do -
   stated preference often differs from revealed preference.

=== FUNDRAISING BASICS ===
- Pre-seed/seed rounds typically fund 12-18 months of runway.
- A cap table tracks ownership percentages across founders, employees,
  and investors; dilution happens with each new funding round.
- SAFEs (Simple Agreements for Future Equity) and convertible notes are
  common early-stage instruments that convert to equity at a future
  priced round, usually with a valuation cap and/or discount.
- Term sheets: key terms to understand include valuation (pre/post-
  money), liquidation preference, board seats, and anti-dilution
  provisions.
- Investors generally evaluate: team, market size (TAM/SAM/SOM),
  traction, and defensibility (moat).
- Bootstrapping (self-funding via revenue) trades slower growth for
  full ownership and control - a valid path, not just "not fundable."

=== GROWTH & MARKETING ===
- AARRR framework (Pirate Metrics): Acquisition, Activation, Retention,
  Referral, Revenue - a funnel for thinking about growth systematically.
- CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) vs LTV (Lifetime Value): a healthy
  business generally needs LTV to be at least 3x CAC.
- Channels to test: content/SEO (slow, compounding), paid ads (fast,
  costly), partnerships, community-led growth, cold outreach.
- Product-led growth: the product itself drives acquisition (referrals,
  virality, freemium conversion) rather than relying solely on sales/
  marketing spend.
- Retention is usually the highest-leverage metric to improve early -
  growth on top of a leaky bucket doesn't compound.

=== COMMON EARLY MISTAKES ===
- Building too much before validating demand ("premature scaling").
- Hiring ahead of revenue/traction, increasing burn rate unsustainably.
- Ignoring unit economics (cost to acquire a customer vs. their
  lifetime value) in pursuit of growth at any cost.
- Chasing too many customer segments at once instead of dominating one
  niche first.
- Co-founder misalignment on equity split, roles, or vision - resolve
  this explicitly and in writing early, not informally.

=== BUSINESS FUNDAMENTALS ===
- Runway: how many months a company can operate before running out of
  cash at current burn rate; a critical number to track weekly/monthly.
- Gross margin: revenue minus cost of goods sold, expressed as a
  percentage - SaaS businesses typically target 70-90%+ gross margins.
- MRR/ARR (Monthly/Annual Recurring Revenue): the standard growth
  metric for subscription businesses.
- Churn: the rate at which customers cancel/stop paying; reducing churn
  is often cheaper than acquiring new customers to replace lost ones.
- Legal basics: choose an entity structure (e.g. LLC, C-Corp) based on
  fundraising plans and tax considerations; formalize IP ownership and
  co-founder agreements early to avoid disputes later.

=== CUSTOMER DISCOVERY ===
- Interview recent real behavior rather than hypothetical intention. Ask
  when the problem last occurred, how it was handled, what it cost, and who
  controls the buying decision.
- Avoid pitching during discovery. Leading questions create compliments,
  not evidence. Strong evidence includes paid pilots, repeated use, referrals,
  signed letters of intent, and customers giving time or data.
- Separate user, buyer, approver, and beneficiary when selling to businesses;
  each may value different outcomes and raise different objections.
- Synthesize interviews into repeated problems, triggers, alternatives, and
  desired outcomes. Do not overgeneralize from one enthusiastic respondent.

=== PRICING AND UNIT ECONOMICS ===
- Price around customer value and willingness to pay, not solely development
  cost. Test packages and price points directly with the target segment.
- Contribution margin is revenue minus variable costs. A business can show
  revenue growth while losing money on every additional transaction.
- CAC payback period measures how long gross profit takes to recover customer
  acquisition cost. Shorter payback reduces working-capital pressure.
- Net revenue retention includes expansion, contraction, and churn from an
  existing customer cohort. Above 100% means expansion exceeds losses.
- Cohort analysis groups customers by start period or behavior and reveals
  whether retention is improving beneath aggregate growth.

=== MVP SCOPE AND PRODUCT ROADMAP ===
- Define the riskiest assumption first, then build the cheapest experiment
  capable of disproving it. An MVP is a learning instrument, not simply a
  low-quality version of the final product.
- Prioritize with expected impact, confidence, effort, and strategic fit.
  Keep a written list of what is explicitly out of scope.
- Instrument activation and retention before launch so learning does not
  depend only on anecdotes.
- A roadmap should communicate problems and desired outcomes, while near-term
  delivery plans may contain specific features and dates.

=== SALES AND GO-TO-MARKET ===
- Choose an initial ideal customer profile with a shared painful problem,
  buying process, budget, and reachable channel.
- Founder-led sales teaches objections and language before the process is
  delegated. Document qualification, discovery, demonstration, proposal,
  negotiation, and follow-up stages.
- Sales-led motion fits complex, high-value products; product-led motion fits
  products users can adopt independently; many businesses use a hybrid.
- Forecast using stage conversion rates and realistic sales-cycle length,
  not the total headline value of every lead in the pipeline.

=== OPERATIONS AND TEAM ===
- Define founder responsibilities, decision rights, vesting, IP assignment,
  compensation expectations, and a process for deadlocks or departures.
- Hire when a recurring bottleneck is understood and valuable work exists,
  not merely because funding arrived. Contractors can validate a role first.
- Maintain a rolling cash forecast with base, upside, and downside scenarios.
  Start fundraising well before cash becomes critical because rounds take time.
