DOMAINLY.AI - GAMING KNOWLEDGE BASE

=== CORE CONCEPTS & TERMINOLOGY ===
- "Meta" refers to the currently most effective strategies/builds in a
  game, shifting as patches and balance updates release.
- "Frame data" in fighting games: startup, active, and recovery frames
  for a move, used to calculate advantage/disadvantage on hit or block.
- "Tick rate" in shooters: how often the server updates game state;
  higher tick rate generally means more accurate hit registration.
- "RNG" (Random Number Generator): the system behind randomized drops,
  crits, and procs; "RNG-heavy" games rely more on luck than skill.
- "Aggro"/"threat": in RPGs/MMOs, the mechanic determining which enemy
  targets which player, often managed by tanks.
- "Ping/latency": delay between input and server response, critical in
  competitive online games; high ping causes rubber-banding and hit-reg
  issues.

=== GENRES OVERVIEW ===
- FPS (First-Person Shooter): reflex and aim-focused, e.g. tactical
  shooters (slower, tactical positioning) vs arena shooters (fast,
  movement-heavy).
- MOBA (Multiplayer Online Battle Arena): team-based, lane-pushing,
  hero/champion progression within a single match.
- RPG (Role-Playing Game): character progression, stats, narrative
  choice; subtypes include ARPGs (action combat) and turn-based RPGs.
- Roguelike/Roguelite: procedurally generated runs, permadeath, meta-
  progression between runs (roguelite) vs none (pure roguelike).
- Battle Royale: last-player/team-standing, shrinking play area, loot-
  based power progression within a single match.
- Strategy (RTS/4X): resource management, base building, or empire-
  building over longer time horizons.

=== IMPROVING AT COMPETITIVE GAMES ===
1. Review your own replays/VODs to identify repeated mistakes rather
   than only watching pros.
2. Focus on fundamentals (aim, positioning, resource/economy management)
   before advanced techniques or niche strategies.
3. Warm up with mechanical drills (aim trainers, practice modes) before
   ranked/competitive play.
4. Take breaks - fatigue measurably reduces reaction time and decision
   quality after extended play sessions.
5. Study matchup-specific knowledge (counters, timings) rather than
   only general mechanical skill.
6. Communicate with teammates concisely in team games - callouts should
   be short and information-dense.

=== GAME DESIGN BASICS ===
- Core loop: the repeated cycle of actions a player performs (e.g. loot,
  fight, upgrade, repeat) that the whole game is built around.
- Difficulty curve should generally ramp gradually, with spikes reserved
  for boss fights or milestone moments, not sustained frustration.
- Player feedback (visual, audio, haptic) should be immediate and clear
  so actions feel responsive ("game feel").
- Reward schedules: variable-ratio rewards (unpredictable timing/size)
  are the most engaging but also the most exploitative if used
  irresponsibly (seen in loot boxes/gacha mechanics).
- Onboarding should teach mechanics through play (guided first levels),
  not walls of text.

=== HARDWARE & PERFORMANCE ===
- Frame rate consistency (low frame-time variance) often matters more
  for how a game "feels" than raw average FPS.
- Input lag comes from multiple sources: display response time,
  peripherals, and the rendering pipeline (V-Sync can add noticeable
  lag; consider G-Sync/FreeSync or capping FPS instead).
- DLSS/FSR (upscaling tech) renders at a lower resolution and uses AI/
  algorithms to upscale, trading a small quality loss for higher FPS.
- CPU vs GPU bottleneck: if GPU usage is near 100% and FPS is capped by
  it, you're GPU-bound; if GPU usage is low but FPS is still low, you're
  likely CPU-bound (common in strategy/simulation games with many units).

=== ESPORTS & MONETIZATION ===
- Esports revenue streams: sponsorships, media rights, ticket sales,
  in-game cosmetic sales tied to tournaments (e.g. battle passes).
- "Free-to-play" (F2P) games typically monetize via cosmetics, battle
  passes, or (in weaker designs) pay-to-win mechanics.
- Live-service games rely on regular content updates (seasons, events)
  to retain player engagement over years rather than a single release.

=== GAME ENGINES (BASICS FOR ASPIRING DEVS) ===
- Unity: C#-based, widely used for 2D/3D indie and mobile games, large
  asset store and community.
- Unreal Engine: C++/Blueprint (visual scripting), strong for high-
  fidelity 3D graphics and AAA-style projects.
- Godot: open-source, lightweight, GDScript (Python-like) or C#,
  popular for 2D and smaller-scale 3D projects.

=== AIM, POSITIONING, AND DECISION MAKING ===
- Crosshair placement reduces the distance needed to adjust when an enemy
  appears. Keep it near likely threat locations and at expected target
  height rather than pointing at the floor.
- Positioning should provide cover, an escape route, and useful sightlines.
  Avoid exposing yourself to several angles simultaneously.
- Isolate fights when outnumbered by using terrain to make opponents engage
  one at a time. Numbers advantage usually matters more than flashy mechanics.
- Track cooldowns, ammunition, economy, and objective timing. Strong players
  win by acting when opponents have fewer resources, not only through aim.
- After each loss, identify one controllable decision to improve. Blaming
  matchmaking or teammates produces little actionable learning.

=== PC PERFORMANCE TROUBLESHOOTING ===
- Measure before changing settings: record frame rate, frame-time graph,
  GPU utilization, CPU-per-core load, temperature, and memory use.
- Stuttering may come from shader compilation, storage access, insufficient
  RAM/VRAM, thermal throttling, background software, or unstable drivers.
- Lower GPU-heavy settings such as resolution, ray tracing, shadows, and
  volumetrics when GPU-bound. Simulation density and view distance often
  affect the CPU more strongly.
- Exclusive fullscreen is not always faster on modern operating systems;
  test fullscreen and borderless modes on the actual game and system.
- Change one setting at a time and keep notes so improvement is attributable.

=== ACCESSIBILITY AND HEALTHY PLAY ===
- Useful accessibility options include remappable controls, subtitles with
  speaker labels, scalable UI, color-blind-safe indicators, aim assistance,
  difficulty options, and reduced motion or flashing effects.
- Ergonomics matter during long sessions: neutral wrist position, supported
  posture, comfortable display height, hydration, and regular movement breaks.
- A sustainable practice schedule combines focused drills, real matches,
  review, sleep, and recovery. Excessive volume with declining attention can
  reinforce mistakes instead of improving skill.

=== TEAMWORK AND COMMUNICATION ===
- Good callouts state location, number of opponents, useful damage/status,
  and direction of travel. Avoid narrating every action or arguing mid-round.
- Before a team engagement, establish the win condition: objective control,
  priority target, resource advantage, or safe disengagement.
- Constructive review discusses observable decisions and alternatives, not a
  teammate's personality. Psychological safety improves team learning.
